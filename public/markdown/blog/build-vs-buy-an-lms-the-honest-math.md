# Build vs. Buy an LMS: The Honest Math

> The right comparison is not a monthly licence against a one-time build. It is three years of subscriptions, staff workarounds, integrations, revenue constraints, and ownership.

## Buy or build, in one line

Buy an LMS when a hosted product already supports the way you sell, teach, and report. Build when the same workaround shows up in revenue, learner access, and staff time—and you can name the business rule the software will never represent cleanly.

Buying is not a failure of ambition. A $69 or $139 monthly plan can be the sensible choice for a proven, straightforward course catalogue. Building too early is an expensive way to avoid learning what customers actually need. But a low subscription number stops being the whole price when someone is exporting completion data every Friday or manually giving access to people who bought the “wrong” bundle.

## Put both options on the same spreadsheet

Do not compare a monthly subscription with a one-time project cost. Compare the cost of operating the learning product for three years.

| Cost line | Buy | Build |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Platform subscription | Monthly or annual licence, often tiered by features, users, or volume | Hosting and third-party services |
| Setup | Configuration, theme, plugins, integrations | Product discovery, design, engineering, testing |
| Exceptions | Staff time, workarounds, plugin maintenance | Product changes you decide to fund |
| Control | Bounded by the vendor model and roadmap | Bounded by your budget and product discipline |
| Exit | Export and migration constraints | Your code, data model, and infrastructure agreement |

Take an ordinary example. Your team sells a $1,200 leadership programme to individuals and companies. The hosted LMS handles individual checkout. It cannot let a company buy 20 seats, invite learners over time, and keep the purchaser separate from the learners without a mixture of coupons, a form, and admin imports. Sales closes the deal; operations creates accounts; support explains why the buyer cannot see the right report.

The licence did not cause the problem. The business outgrew its model.

## Count the work around the product

Write down every action that starts outside the LMS and ends with somebody correcting data inside it. Five minutes does not sound costly until it happens across four people and every new cohort.

| Weekly workaround | Hours per week | Annual hours | What it usually hides |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Reconcile paid enrolments | 2 | 104 | Payment and access are not connected |
| Import team learners | 3 | 156 | The sales model does not match the user model |
| Rebuild a client report | 2 | 104 | Reporting permissions are too generic |
| Answer access exceptions | 2 | 104 | Rules live in people’s heads |

That example totals **468 hours a year**. Put a loaded internal cost against those hours. Then add the things a time sheet misses: a learner waiting after payment, a sales promise that cannot be made with confidence, or an operations lead avoiding a new offer because delivery will become painful.

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the May 2024 median annual wage for software developers at **$133,080**. It is a labour-market figure, not a build quote, but it explains why custom work has a real upfront price: good software is a multidisciplinary job. [The underlying BLS figure is here.](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/computer-and-information-technology/software-developers.htm)

## The honest case for buying

Hosted platforms win when the product is standard and speed matters more than special behaviour. Use one when you are validating a course, your access rules are simple, and the vendor has the integrations you actually need. You get mature billing, account recovery, and platform maintenance without owning the whole problem.

That can remain true for years. A creator selling three self-paced courses with one-time purchase may be better served by a hosted product plus a sharp support process than by a $50,000 build. Do not use custom software to make a normal course catalogue feel more important.

## The honest case for building

Custom starts to earn its keep when the platform itself changes what you can sell or deliver. The trigger might be enterprise seat management, a regulated evidence trail, a multi-tenant academy, a workflow tied to a proprietary learning method, or an integration that has to be reliable because another system owns the source of truth.

The first release should prove that one advantage. At Rizon, I would rather build the employer-seat flow properly than include a broad “community” feature because every LMS competitor has one. Software stays maintainable when the first version is allowed to be specific.

## Make the call with a break-even question

Ask this: **What must become true for a custom build to repay its first-release cost?**

For a $45,000 build, the answer might be 40 additional programme sales at $1,200, a saved half-time operations role, or retention that improves because learners stop falling through broken handoffs. If you cannot name a credible path, wait. Fix the process, simplify the offer, or stay hosted for another cycle.
