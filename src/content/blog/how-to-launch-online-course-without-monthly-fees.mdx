The creator economy sold everyone on the same dream: sign up for a platform, upload your content, start earning. What the landing pages do not mention is that you are entering a long-term revenue-sharing arrangement that gets more expensive the more successful you become.

There is a different path. It requires more upfront thinking, but it eliminates the monthly drag on your margins indefinitely.

## Why the Default Advice Is Expensive

The standard advice for launching an online course is to pick Teachable, Thinkific, or Kajabi. They are fast to set up, handle payments, and require no technical knowledge. For testing an idea with zero students, that is reasonable.

The problem is that most creators never revisit the decision once they have momentum. They stay on the platform that launched them, even as the fees compound.

Here is what that actually looks like:

- Kajabi's Growth plan: $199/month — $2,388/year
- Teachable's Pro plan: $119/month plus 0% transaction fees, but you still pay the annual subscription
- Thinkific's Start plan: $74/month, but limits you to one community and basic quizzes

These numbers are before payment processing fees (Stripe adds another 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction).

## The Alternative: Own Your Infrastructure

A custom course platform — built once, hosted on infrastructure you control — flips the cost model entirely.

Your ongoing costs become:
- **Hosting:** $50–150/month for a platform handling thousands of concurrent learners
- **Payment processing:** Stripe or Paddle at standard rates, nothing else on top
- **Domain and email:** Under $20/month

That is it. No subscription. No revenue share. No per-student fee.

## What a Custom Platform Actually Requires

Building a course platform is not a weekend project, but it is also not as complex as most people assume. The core pieces are:

**Authentication and user management** — students sign up, log in, and have accounts. Standard work.

**Course and content structure** — modules, lessons, video hosting (usually delegated to Moodle, Vimeo, or a CDN). The platform organizes and gates content based on enrollment and progress.

**Payment and enrollment** — a checkout flow connected to Stripe. On successful payment, the student gets access. Straightforward with modern tools.

**Progress tracking** — marking lessons complete, tracking quiz scores, issuing certificates. This is where the real pedagogical value lives.

**Admin dashboard** — you need to see who enrolled, how far they have gotten, and what revenue looks like. The dashboard your students never see but you use every day.

None of these are novel engineering problems. They have been solved many times. The work is assembling them in a way that fits how you teach.

## The Economics Over Time

Assume you build a custom platform for $25,000 — a realistic number for a well-built, production-ready course site. You were previously on a $199/month SaaS plan with 2% revenue sharing, and you do $150,000 in annual course sales.

Year 1:
- SaaS cost: $2,388 + $3,000 (2% of $150k) = **$5,388**
- Custom platform: $25,000 build + $1,200 hosting = **$26,200**

Year 2:
- SaaS: **$5,388**
- Custom: **$1,200**

Year 3 and beyond:
- SaaS: **$5,388/year**
- Custom: **$1,200/year**

Break-even: roughly 27 months. Everything after that is $4,188 per year back in your pocket — plus the compounding benefit that as your revenue grows, your platform costs stay flat.

## What to Look For in a Development Partner

Not every agency builds learning platforms. When evaluating who to build with, look for:

**Domain experience.** Have they built LMS products before? Do they understand concepts like course completion, prerequisite gating, and certificate logic? General web shops learn on your budget.

**Code ownership as a default.** Some shops build on proprietary frameworks or lock you into their hosting. You want a partner who hands you the code at the end.

**Ongoing support options, but no lock-in.** The platform should be buildable and maintainable by any competent developer, not just the team who built it.

**Clear scope definition.** Scope creep is where custom projects go over budget. A good partner will scope the project tightly and flag changes explicitly.

## Starting Without Going All-In

You do not have to build everything at once. A sensible first phase might include:

1. Authentication and student accounts
2. Course catalog and content delivery
3. Stripe checkout and enrollment
4. Basic progress tracking

Launch with that. Once you have real learners using it, you have real feedback to guide what to build next. Certificates, cohort scheduling, community features, analytics dashboards — these all come later, informed by actual usage rather than speculation.

The goal is not to build the perfect platform before launch. It is to own the infrastructure from day one, then grow into it.
